Canada's culture embodies the artistic, culinary, literary, comedic, musical, political, and social characteristics that are characteristic to Canadians. Throughout its history, Canada's culture has been affected by European culture and customs, particularly those of the British and French, as well as indigenous cultures. Elements of the cultures of Canada's immigrant communities have been assimilated through time to produce a Canadian cultural mosaic. Because of similar language (in English-speaking Canada), extensive media penetration, and physical proximity, some portions of Canada's population have been impacted by American culture to varied degrees.

Canada is frequently described as "extremely progressive, diverse, and multicultural". Because of its public emphasis on the social value of immigration, Canada's federal government has sometimes been labelled as the originator of multicultural ism. Canada's culture is influenced by its diverse ethnicities, and measures that promote a fair society are constitutionally protected. Canadian government policies, such as publicly funded health care, higher and more progressive taxation, the abolition of the death penalty, strong efforts to eliminate poverty, an emphasis on cultural diversity, strict gun control, and the legalisation of same-sex marriage, pregnancy terminations, euthanasia, and cannabis, are social indicators of the country's political and cultural values. Canadians connect with the country's health-care institutions, military peacekeeping, national park system, and the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.

With programmes, regulations, and organisations, the Canadian government has affected culture. It has established crown companies to promote Canadian culture through media, such as the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation (CBC) and the National Film Board of Canada (NFB), and it supports a variety of events that it believes enhance Canadian customs. It has also attempted to conserve Canadian culture by establishing legal minimums for Canadian content in a variety of media through organisations such as the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC).
